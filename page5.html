NIGERIA

One of the earliest Nigerian made comic books was Wale Adenuga’s Ikebe Super and Super Story — which was a big deal in the 70s and 80s. The advent of Supa Strikas in 2001 ushered in a refined version of Nigerian comics.

The Nigerian comic book industry faced challenges during the late 1990s and early 2000s, with a decline in printed comics due to economic factors and a shift toward digital entertainment. However, in the 2000s, there was a resurgence in Nigerian comics, largely fueled by a new generation of creators who embraced digital media, webcomics, and social media platforms. Creators like Akinola Davis Jr. ("Kala") and Jide Martin ("CelestialVibe") began to gain recognition. The emergence of platforms like "Comic Republic" and "YouNeek Studios" has been instrumental in promoting Nigerian comics and graphic novels, featuring characters like "Guardian Prime" and "Malika: Warrior Queen.

SOUTH AFRICA

Comics in South Africa have a long history, dating back to the early 20th century, with the emergence of humor strips and political cartoons in local newspapers. However, the medium truly came into its own during the latter part of the 20th century and into the 21st century. Pioneering artists and writers began producing comics that addressed a wide range of themes, including apartheid, social justice, folklore, and everyday life. These comics became a potent tool for cultural preservation and political commentary. Despite the challenges of censorship during apartheid, the industry continued to thrive, with the emergence of local publishing houses, comic conventions, and an array of diverse voices. South African comics have gained international recognition, blending traditional storytelling elements with modern styles to create unique and engaging narratives.

ZIMBABWE

The history of comics in Zimbabwe is a testament to the country's resilience, creativity, and evolving artistic expression. Comics in Zimbabwe have a relatively recent history, with the medium gaining prominence in the latter half of the 20th century. These comics emerged during a period of significant political and social change, offering a platform for artists to address complex issues and tell stories that resonated with the local audience. Despite challenges like economic instability and limited access to resources, Zimbabwean comic creators persevered. Their works, often characterized by a unique blend of traditional storytelling, contemporary themes, and socio-political commentary, found recognition both locally and internationally. As technology and digital platforms became more accessible, Zimbabwean comics saw a resurgence, with creators finding new ways to share their stories with a global audience.

KENYA

The history of comics in Kenya is a vibrant and evolving narrative that reflects the country's rich cultural diversity and artistic expression. Comics in Kenya have a history dating back to the mid-20th century, with early examples often found in newspapers and magazines, primarily serving as humor or political satire. The true growth of the Kenyan comic scene, however, gained momentum in the late 20th century and early 21st century. Pioneering artists and writers started creating comics that explored a wide range of themes, including social issues, folklore, history, and contemporary life. The emergence of local comic conventions and independent publishers played a crucial role in nurturing the comic culture. Kenyan comics have been a platform for cultural preservation and storytelling, with creators embracing both traditional and contemporary influences to produce engaging and diverse narratives.

UGANDA

The history of comics in Uganda is a relatively recent but rapidly growing phenomenon. Comics as a form of storytelling and art have gained prominence in Uganda over the past few decades. While the country does not have an extensive tradition of comic book creation dating back centuries, it has embraced this visual medium as a powerful means of storytelling and expression in the modern era. The growth of Ugandan comics can be attributed to the increasing interest in visual arts, the influence of global pop culture, and the desire to share and preserve Ugandan stories. As technology and connectivity have advanced, Ugandan comic creators have found new opportunities to share their work with a broader audience, both domestically and internationally.
